Jinnah formally entered politics in 1905 from the platform of the Indian National Congress. Political Career

Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah had a long and illustrious political career, which spanned over several decades. Jinnah began his political career in the early 20th century as a member of the Indian National Congress and worked towards Hindu-Muslim unity. However, after growing disillusioned with the Congress's policies, he joined the All India Muslim League in 1913 and became its leader in 1934.


Jinnah's leadership of the Muslim League was instrumental in the creation of Pakistan, which he saw as the only solution to protect the rights and interests of the Muslim community in the subcontinent. He worked tirelessly to secure the rights of Muslims and to establish a separate Muslim homeland, which ultimately led to the creation of Pakistan in 1947.

Pakistan resolution

The Lahore Resolution, also known as the Pakistan Resolution, was a significant political declaration passed on March 23, 1940, at the All India Muslim League meeting held in Lahore, British India. The resolution called for the creation of an independent Muslim state, which eventually became Pakistan, comprising Muslim-majority areas in the subcontinent.


The Lahore Resolution was a crucial turning point in the history of the subcontinent as it provided the ideological basis for the Muslim League's demand for a separate homeland for Muslims. The resolution was a culmination of years of political struggle and demands for Muslim representation in the government and legislative bodies. Today, the Lahore Resolution is celebrated as Pakistan Day in the country, and it remains a symbol of the aspirations and struggles of the Muslim community in the subcontinent.

Pakistan Independence

Pakistan's independence in 1947 was a historic moment for the subcontinent, marking the end of British colonial rule and the birth of a new nation. The independence movement, led by the All India Muslim League and its leader, Muhammad Ali Jinnah, called for the creation of a separate Muslim homeland in the subcontinent. The British government, in response to mounting pressure from Indian nationalist movements, agreed to the creation of two separate states, India and Pakistan. On August 14, 1947, Pakistan came into existence, and Jinnah became its first Governor-General.


The event was celebrated with great enthusiasm and joy throughout the country, with people taking to the streets to celebrate their newfound independence. However, the creation of Pakistan was not without its challenges.

Death




The death of Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah, the founder of Pakistan, was a significant loss for the nation. Jinnah passed away on September 11, 1948, in Karachi, after battling with lung cancer. His death came just over a year after Pakistan's independence and was a huge blow to the newly formed country. Jinnah's leadership and vision were instrumental in the creation of Pakistan, and he remained an important figure in the country's early years.


He was a champion of Muslim rights and worked tirelessly for the establishment of a separate Muslim homeland. Jinnah's death was mourned throughout Pakistan, and he remains a revered figure in the country's history. His legacy lives on in the Constitution of Pakistan, which is based on the principles of democracy, freedom, and equality that he championed. The anniversary of Jinnah's death is observed as a national day of mourning in Pakistan, and his memory is celebrated every year with great reverence and respect.
